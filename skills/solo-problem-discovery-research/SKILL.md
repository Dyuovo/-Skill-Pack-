# Solo Problem Discovery Research

## Purpose

Reduce the biggest uncertainty before product definition. Keep discovery cheap, focused, and decision-oriented.

## Inputs

- Idea intake or problem hypothesis.
- Target user segment.
- Known alternatives or current workaround.
- Evidence already available.
- Decision deadline or timebox.

## Workflow

1. State the riskiest assumption.
2. Choose the smallest evidence path:
   - Interview or message 3-5 likely users.
   - Review support logs, personal workflow notes, or community posts.
   - Inspect competitors or substitutes.
   - Run a landing-page, concierge, spreadsheet, or manual prototype probe.
3. Capture functional, emotional, and operational pain.
4. Separate "interesting" from "must solve now."
5. Summarize alternatives: do nothing, spreadsheet/manual, competitor, internal hack.
6. Decide whether to proceed, narrow, park, or reject.

## Research Notes

- Browse or verify current facts when market, competitors, pricing, tools, APIs, or regulations could have changed.
- Prefer official sources for technical/platform claims.
- Do not overfit to one enthusiastic user.
- For solo projects, a strong personal pain can be valid evidence, but label it as such.

## Quality Gate

Move to product definition only when there is enough evidence to name:

- Who has the problem.
- When it happens.
- What they do today.
- Why current alternatives are insufficient.
- What success would look like.
